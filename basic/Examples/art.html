Beyond the floating world: Japanese Prints after Ukiyo-e

Sosaku and Shin Hanga, Nov 4th, 2020

Ukiyo-e roughly translates to "images of the floating world". It traditionally refers to the prints and paintings of 17th to 19th century Japan that predominantly focused on depicting the activities of the pleasure districts (theatre, sumo and courtesans) in Tokyo, as well as scenes from history/folklore and landscapes.

The Ukiyo-e tradition faded towards the end of the 19th century. It successors in the early 20th were heavily influenced by Japans exposure to western artistic traditions. In the world of printmaking two distinct groups of print artists formed, the "Creative Prints" or "Sosaku Hanga" and "New Prints" or "Shin Hanga". The creative prints made liberal use of western techniques and were the predominant group of abstract print artists in the country. They departed from traditional methods of Japanese printmaking where prints were produced by a group of distinct professionals - designers creating images, carvers cutting them and printers producing the images enmass. Instead these creative print artists were often responsible for the whole process and thus the entirety of the output. By contrast the "New" print artists actually sought to create new prints in the old ukiyoe fashion, it was an attempt to revive the techniques and traditions of the past in the new era. The main distinction between the New Prints and the Ukiyo-e of the past was that while the New Prints picked up on the same genres it did incorporate the western techniques and perspectives that weren't known previously.

De Stjil - The two pillars of the art movement

Piet Mondrian and Theo van Doesburg, Nov 1st, 2020

Two of the chief figures behind the De Stijl movement.

Piet Mondriaan (1872-1944) and Theo van Doesburg (1883-1931) both born in the Netherlands co-founded De Stijl, which was originally a publication, in 1917. It was committed to strictly geometrical art in black, white or primary colours.
